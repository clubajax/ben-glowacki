MVCC speaker: Diversity is not ‘noticing the differences’

Kathleen Duncan / Observer-Dispatch
Matt Glowacki speaks to a theater full of students at MVCC Wednesday November 16, 2011. Glowacki's speech was titled "Diversity according to South park & Family Guy." He spoke and showed clips pertaining to issues such as society's idea of beauty, respect for those who are differently abled and having self confidence. "We all have the ability to be the most important person in someone else's life, by seeing their potential" he said. "It comes down to another person telling you, yes you can."
By CASSAUNDRA BABER
Observer-Dispatch
Posted Nov 16, 2011 @ 05:58 PM
UTICA —

Every day, 38-year-old Matt Glowacki wakes up happy.

It can be seen in the way a smile spreads across his face, the way he moves – almost bouncing – or the way he races to hold doors open for others.

Still, he told students at Mohawk Valley Community College, people say to him: “You’re so happy. It’s amazing. If I was like you … I’d have to kill myself.”

Usually, those people are looking toward Glowacki’s lap – to where his legs would be if he hadn’t been born without them.

“If I had to walk around my life as ignorant as you are, I’d have to take my own life, too,” Glowacki normally responds, smile blazing.

In an effort to curtail that ignorance, business owner and Paralympics athlete Glowacki travels the country as a diversity speaker, sharing how focusing on each others’ differences actually divides people.

He made those points to a full theater in MVCC’s Information Technology Building on Wednesday afternoon by mixing statistics with his own comedic life experiences and clips from “The Family Guy” and “South Park.”

The adult cartoons typically have come under fire for political incorrectness, but Glowacki contends that the shows actually break down stereotypes.

“Diversity is not walking around noticing the differences in people,” Glowacki said to the crowd of mostly college students.

Things would be different if we allowed ourselves to have conversations with people who are different from us, he said. But political correctness can stop those conversations. The politically correct label, “person with a disability,” for example, is a disservice, he said.

“All those words set up a lower expectation of what you can do before you get to know someone,” Glowacki said.

The lecture, titled “Diversity according to South Park and Family Guy,” also focused on issues of body image, a subject particularly relevant among college students, he said, referring to statistics that point to college women as most affected by eating disorders.

Near the end of the talk, Glowacki challenged the audience to expect more from people with disabilities. And to realize that even positive comments can be insulting at times.

“When you ask a question or make a comment (to someone with a disability), I want you to have it come from a high expectation,” he said. “‘Wow, it’s amazing you can drive a car, because you’re in a wheelchair’ sounds a lot like ‘Wow, it’s amazing you’re in college because you’re a woman.’”
